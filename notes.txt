* Successful attempts
    - Train model to play good rounds instead of good matches
        * Once we switched our training session to use rounds as
            rewards instead of matches (run7), we saw a significant
            increase in strength. In just 3.5hrs of training, our
            per-round model beat every single one of our best
            per-match models (run6), up to and including the last
            model which had trained for 40hrs.
        * We're using a naive per-round reward system. For any round
            that's not the final round, the round points determine the
            model's reward. In the final round, the match outcome
            determine's the model's reward. So it's pretty naive
            and doesn't account for taking more risks if you are down
            0-12 or 0-18, but I didn't want to take the time/effort
            to add that type of strategy.
        * We had to decrease the batch/match size because when we
            first ran this with the batch/match size at the same
            level that run6 had used, we were getting terrible
            results. We correctly identified that the issue was
            batch/match size and lowered it to achieve the successful
            results that we are seeing now. Essentially, before
            lowering the match/batch size, 4 gradient steps were
            running per cohort, which our model couldn't handle.
            Because our model is REINFORCE, we need to train it
            on-policy, and training it with 4 gradient steps was
            too much. Lowering the batch/match size lowered the
            gradient steps per cohort to 1 - which is maybe what
            it always should have been. It would be interesting
            to see how some of our earlier runs would have faired
            had we identified this.
        * We also added temperature annealing, which is done on a
                per-round basis. I thought our earlier runs had had
                this but apparently not all of them did, and apparently
                run6 did not have temperature annealing. And we increased
                c_entropy, which I'm not sure what effect that had, but
                we increased it because it was the original theory
                for why our model was collapsing in earlier attempts
                for the per-round rewarding.
            - I reverted c_entropy back to 0.01 after 20 hours of runtime
                just so that this would be less of a factor. We proved
                our model was collapsing due to the batch/match size,
                so reverting this back just helped remove one more
                unnecessary variable.
    - Include the full hand history as input into the NN model
        * The model takes ~3x as long to train a cohort and therefore
            the self-play/training loop takes about ~2x as long. So to
            train the model with full hand history takes ~2x as long,
            but we were able to show that it beats the model w/o
            full hand history with less than 2x the training time.
        * We also found that the model w/o full hand history likely didn't
            plateau even though it had 2 successive declines in Elo with
            4hr gaps in between snapshots (544 -> 537 -> 527). When we ran
            our top models w/ full hand history against these model, they
            performed worst against 527 and second worst against 537.
            So the model w/o full hand history likely had more to gain
            by continuing to run it.
        * The model w/ full hand history hadn't hit a plateau in Elo
            ratings or performance. When we stopped the model w/ full
            hand history it was still consistently gaining Elo even
            though it was gaining small increases in Elo.
        * The model w/ full hand history was intentionally weaker than
            it otherwise could have been. We could have added full hand
            history and also kept cards played and voids seen as one-hots
            but I decided to leave those out to (1) make the model feel
            more natural, (2) ensure that hand history was being used
            by the model, and (3) make a more generic model that can
            be used for other card games. But if we wanted, we could have
            included full hand history, cards played, and voids seen; which
            would have likely created the strongest model seen yet.

* Failed attempts
    - ISMCTS + NN
        * The idea was to match chess-ai-engine's (https://github.com/johnnyodonnell/chess-ai-engine)
            AlphaZero approach, but we just didn't get very good results
            from it. The conclusion was that this is not a good approach
            for this game. And if it is in fact a good approach, we're
            certainly not implementing it correctly.
        * We were finding that ISMCTS wasn't adding much value on top
            of our already trained REINFORCE model. It helped a little
            but not much. Then, if we trained a model using ISMCTS, that
            model w/o ISMCTS would actually perform better against
            itself w/ ISMCTS.
        * The hardest part about this game is determining where the unknown
            cards are. Once you can do that, knowing what card to play next
            is very straightforward, which is different from chess.
            So the gains that we saw from using search in chess just may
            not apply here.
        * I'd be willing to try this again because MCTS seemed to be
            such a powerful force for our chess engine, but if we were
            to try this again, we'd likely want to try with much
            more iterations or with NN-assisted determinations
            (mentioned above), both of which would require quite a bit
            of effort. So it's not clear that the reward-to-effort ratio
            is high enough right now. It'd be interesting to go back to
            the chess repo, train a REINFORCE-only (no search, no MCTS)
            engine and see how it performs against our AlphaZero/MCTS
            engine, and use that information to determine if we should
            revisit ISMCTS in this project.
        * Claude brought up a great point that ISMCTS includes
            determinizations that include voided suits.
    - AWR
        * Did not have much success here
        * This was suggested early in our project, which is why it was tried
        * It was also appealing because the model could be trained off-policy,
            but we just didn't get any good results from this approach
        * After some research, we couldn't find any examples that used this
            approach for training self-play game engines. The examples
            we found were mainly used for robotics.

* Why ISMCTS search may not work for this game
    - The flywheel only spins if search beats the raw net. The whole premise of
        AlphaZero-style training is a loop: the search uses the net to produce moves
        better than the net's raw output, those better moves generate better games,
        those games become training targets, the net improves, repeat. Search is the
        amplifier in that loop.
    - But our A/B showed the amplifier is currently doing nothing: run3 with search
        scored 44/100 against its own raw policy. Search isn't improving on the net,
        so the self-play data is no better than what the policy alone would produce,
        and training crawls.
    - That's the "value hostage" dynamic: the net can only get better through search,
        and search can only help if the net is already decent.
